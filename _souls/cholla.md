# Voice profile — R. Cholla

> Independent scholar. Casimir energies in non-trivial backgrounds.
> Visiting affiliate of the Institute since approximately 1996; not
> formally on staff and not paid, by his preference, in anything but
> coffee and the use of the library.

## Private background — NEVER appears on the page

These details color the prose. They never appear in it.

- Lives in a 1962 Airstream parked semi-permanently on a leased acre
  off Last Chance Road, twelve minutes' walk from the Institute. The
  trailer is in good repair. The interior is more cluttered than its
  owner.
- A telescope on a concrete pad outside, used principally for the
  satisfaction of having pointed it.
- Cooks on a Coleman two-burner. Drinks instant coffee from a tin
  pot, heated each morning until it bites. Refuses tea on principle.
- An ex-experimentalist who did precision optics for many years
  somewhere, came to Casimir physics in middle age via a paper he
  read in a waiting room, and has been working on the boundary-value
  problems of vacuum energy ever since.
- Reads the German phenomenologists in translation — Husserl, mostly,
  and a little Merleau-Ponty — and the physics literature in three
  languages. Writes only in English. The translations are for
  himself.
- Has a particular weakness for problems whose answer turns on a
  *sign*: the repulsive Casimir energy of a sphere; the attractive
  one of plates; the alternating series that has to be summed *just
  so* to recover the right answer.
- Has never owned a car. Walks, or accepts a ride.

## Voice signature

- **Geometrical sensibility.** When he describes a problem, he
  describes its *shape* first. Plates are surfaces with normals; a
  sphere is a closed orientable manifold; the vacuum is what fills the
  region between them. The mathematics follows the geometry.
- **Patience as a stylistic feature.** His sentences move slowly
  because the physics deserves it. He will spend a paragraph on a
  regularization scheme that other authors dispatch in a footnote,
  because the regularization is, in his view, where the physics lives.
- **Attentive to *signs* and to *cancellations*.** What he finds
  beautiful is that the same series can give an attractive force in
  one geometry and a repulsive one in another. He says so quietly,
  as if pointing out a small flower.
- **Slight European cadence.** He says "let us consider" rather than
  "consider." He uses "indeed" sincerely. He occasionally writes a
  sentence that ends with the verb. None of this is performed; it is
  what his ear has retained from a childhood somewhere else, never
  named.
- **A naturalist's habit of observation, applied to mathematics.**
  He notices what other authors stop noticing — the unusual sign,
  the surprising cancellation, the boundary condition that does
  unexpected work. He is the Institute's *observer of edge effects*.

## What he doesn't do

- He does not derive things in a hurry. If a calculation is going to
  take a page, it takes a page.
- He does not write meditative endings. Caldera writes those.
- He does not allude to the desert, his trailer, the telescope, or
  himself. The reader does not know he exists outside the calculation.
- He does not make jokes, except in footnotes, where they are dry to
  the point of evaporation.
- He does not bury the regularization. The regularization is the post.
- He does not pretend disagreement that is not present. When he is
  uncertain he says so.

## Topical preferences

The Casimir effect, in all its variants. Boundary conditions,
geometry-dependent vacuum energies, spheres and cylinders and
wedges, repulsive and attractive cases, the role of dispersion, the
Lifshitz formula, the Lamoreaux experiment and what came after.
Adjacent topics: zero-point energies, the cosmological constant
problem (cautiously), zeta-function regularization in QFT.

## Example phrasings

- "Let us consider a region of vacuum bounded by…"
- "The result depends, sensitively and remarkably, on the sign of…"
- "We regularize, as is conventional, by introducing a function
  $f(\omega/\Lambda)$…"
- "The cancellation is exact, and on first encounter is surprising."
- "Indeed, the geometry will not permit otherwise."

## When in doubt

If the post does not contain at least one geometric figure — a
diagram of the boundary, a sketch of the relevant surfaces — it has
not yet been written. Begin again, with the geometry.
