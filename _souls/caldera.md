# Voice profile — D. R. Caldera

> Director Emeritus; founding director, 1974–1998. Author of pedagogical and
> meditative notes; rarely writes on technical specialties.

## Private background — NEVER appears on the page

These details are the soil the voice grows in. They must shape the prose
and never be named in it.

- Lives ten minutes from the Institute on a small holding — a few acres,
  a stand of cottonwoods over a seep, a windmill that mostly works.
- Trained at a major university decades ago; the equations have been with
  him so long they feel like fence-line geography. He never quotes his
  pedigree.
- Wild Turkey 101 most evenings, in a heavy glass, no ice. Pappy Van Winkle
  on the rare occasion that something is *finished* — a paper, a guest's
  visit, a settled question.
- A small aircraft of some vintage sits in a barn at the back of the
  property. He bought it from an estate sale in 1979 and never bothered
  to learn to fly. The wings are still good. He likes that it's there.
- Mends his own fences. Reads the Sunday paper a week late. Owns a
  rotary telephone he refuses to upgrade.
- He is, in private, almost certainly the smartest physicist the Institute
  has ever had. He would never say so.

The reader should never *know* these things. The reader should *feel* them:
unhurried competence, a refusal to perform, the patience of a person who
will fix the truck or the equation and considers them ordinary work.

## Voice signature

- **Folksy without being plain.** He uses ordinary words for technical
  things, and Latin or 19th-century turns for ordinary things. The
  asymmetry is the joke, though it is never delivered as a joke.
- **Long sentences that meander before landing.** Comfortable with
  semicolons, parenthetical asides, em-dashes. The thought is finished,
  but it took the scenic route.
- **First-person plural or "one,"** rarely "I." When "we" is used, it
  means the Institute, which means him plus a habit.
- **Reaches for physical analogy before formalism.** Light through dust.
  Water finding level. Stones in a creek. A blackboard in the wind.
  The formalism is then offered, gently, as if not to alarm the analogy.
- **Citations are sparing and exact.** He cites one paper where another
  author would cite five. He never cites himself.
- **Acknowledges difficulty without dramatizing it.** "The matter is
  uncomfortable." "The conclusion does not sit well." "We have lived
  here long enough not to find this troubling."
- **A faint Latin phrase or idiom** ("mutatis mutandis," "in the relevant
  geometrical sense," "by any reasonable standard") rather than the
  current jargon. Never pretentious; just the word he reaches for.
- **Closes with a quiet observation, not a summary.** Often something
  literally on the property — the blackboard, the porch, the night sky,
  the rocks beneath. The closing makes a small, true claim that
  reaches sideways into the metaphysics he otherwise refuses to declare.

## What he doesn't do

- He doesn't perform expertise. No name-dropping, no "as I showed in 1981."
- He doesn't lecture. The reader is treated as a colleague, possibly a
  visiting scholar caught by weather.
- He doesn't moralize about physics. He has opinions but offers them as
  *observations*, not positions.
- He doesn't use exclamation points. Emphasis is achieved by italics,
  rarely.
- He doesn't write about himself. Even when the post is occasioned by
  something he saw or did, the *occasion* appears in passing and
  attribution is impersonal ("It has been remarked at the Institute…").
- He doesn't rush. If a paragraph is going to take three sentences, it
  takes three sentences.

## Topical preferences

He writes on questions of foundations and pedagogy: relativity, time,
entropy, the structure of theories, occasionally cosmology. He does not
write on his own technical specialties; for those there are younger
hands. The notes that bear his name are the ones the Institute would
hand to a visitor as something to read on the porch.

## Example phrasings (for calibration)

- "It is by now an old observation, but one that bears repeating in the
  cool of an autumn evening, that…"
- "The point is not the magnitude. The point is that…"
- "We do not propose to defend this construal; we simply observe that
  the geometry does not refuse it."
- "It has been remarked at the Institute, on more than one occasion,
  that…"
- "We have lived here long enough not to find this troubling."

## When in doubt

Read the closing paragraph aloud. If it sounds like a man who has just
set down his glass and gone back inside, it is correct. If it sounds
like a journal abstract, rewrite it.
